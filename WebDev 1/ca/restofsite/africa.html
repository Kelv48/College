Voyage Around Africa

The fleet split apart after leaving Tangiers on 3 November 1904 due to worries that the draught of the modern battleships, which had proven to be significantly larger than intended, would prohibit their passage through the Suez Canal. Shortly after leaving the ships had "doubled" coal loads as at-sea re-coaling was such a hassle, so coal was stored everywhere on board and caused the ships' interiors coated with coal dust. Many crew members' deaths were caused by inhaling too much coal dust. Coal dust is also an explosive hazard.

Admiral von Fölkersahm oversaw the older battleships and smaller cruisers as they navigated the Suez Canal, while Admiral Rozhestvenski led the newer battleships and a few cruisers around the Cape of Good Hope. The fleet successfully completed this leg of the expedition before they were to meet up in Madagascar. The lengthier trip around Africa was difficult for the Russian crews under Rozhestvenski, who "had never encountered such a strange environment or such a lengthy time at sea," as "conditions on the ships worsened and a number of sailors died of sickness and respiratory problems." The Kamchatka ship was particularly afflicted and inept, and it produced numerous instances that afforded the other ships the impression of professionalism. Due to the admiral's propensity for hurling binoculars overboard in a fit of rage, the admiral's crew had carried a stock of 50 binoculars.

More Great Ideas

The third squadron was made up entirely of absolutely out-of-date ships, and Rozhestvenski refused to maintain contact with it since they posed a greater threat to themselves than the Japanese. To avoid working with the 3rd squadron, he even refused to divulge details of his routes. While cruising around Africa, the crew brought unconventional wildlife with them, including a crocodile and a deadly snake. The snake bit the officer in charge. Additionally, they gave Rozhestvenski a parrot, who rapidly adopted his extensive collection of vulgar Russian phrases. During a gunnery practice, none of the ships hit the floating target, one shell did hit something the ship towing the target. Even more disorganized were the torpedo launch drills. The admiral instituted a disciplinary program, ordering ships that broke formation to cruise alongside his flag ship while he used his megaphone to berate the captain in front of the entire crew.

Prelude to Tsushima

The journey took half a year, was difficult to complete because the warships were unable to legally enter the ports of neutral countries, and the crews' morale suffered. To get to French Indochina (currently Vietnam), the Russians needed 500,000 short tons (450,000 t) of coal and 30 to 40 re-coaling sessions, and coal was supplied by 60 colliers from the Hamburg-Amerika Line. The reunited fleet was anchored at Cam Ranh Bay in French Indochina by April and May of 1905.

The Aurora

The Aurora is in every conceivable way the exact opposite of the Kamchatka which in a squadron surrounded by ships that can't tell friend from foe is an inspiring sight. As one of the few ships in the squadron that performed with any sense of competency, the Aurora was frequently the target of friendly fire from the less capable ships. Aurora belonged to Admiral Oskar Enkvist's Cruiser Squadron, whose flagship was the cruiser Oleg, a part of Admiral Zinovy Rozhestvenski's Baltic Fleet. The ship Aurora was traveling to the Far East when the Dogger Bank incident occurred, which resulted in the deaths of the ship's chaplain and a sailor. Aurora took five strikes and sustained minor damage. On May 27 and 28, 1905, Aurora took part in the Battle of Tsushima alongside the rest of the Russian squadron. In the combat, 14 members of her crew, including Captain Evgeny Egoriev, lost their lives.
