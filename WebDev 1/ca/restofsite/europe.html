European Endeavors

A destroyer collided with another battleship, and the flag ship lost its anchor before they even setsail. A Danish coal ship that had supplied them was rammed. Later while sailing near Denmark an icebreakership was manoeuvring so poorly that the flag ship fired several rounds across its bows for disobeying orders to stop, and the ship was sent home. These series of blunders didn't inspire Rozhestvenski with much confidence and sets the theme for the rest of the voyage. There were rumours going around that made many of the crew members believe that there was a Japanese torpedo-boat fleet, in the Baltic Sea, about 18,000 miles away from Japan, and were also paranoid about Japanese mines. "Another interesting fact they ignored is not only can torpedo boats not sail this extreme distance neither does Japan have enough to make up what would constitute a fleet.” A Russian message ship was mistaken for Japanese torpedo boats and fired upon at one point. The messenger informed Rozhestvenski that he had been promoted to Vice-Admiral.

The North Sea

They later fired on British fishing vessels after mistaking them for torpedo boats and on each other in the confusion. The Aurora, one of the Russian ships, was hit by friendly fire; this despite the fact that it was frequently hit by friendly fire throughout the voyage and was known for being the most capable ship in the fleet and, in a sense, the light in the darkness. Some of the ships claimed over the radio that they thought torpedoes were hitting them, and one ship claimed that they were being boarded with its crew getting on life vests and getting ready to repel boarders with rapiers. About 500 rounds were fired from one ship, but none of them hit the target or a target they simply didn’t hit anything. When the admiral realized what was happening, he gave the order to stop firing, which was disregarded by everyone else. They escaped the scene after realizing what they had done.

Britain's Response

28 cruisers and battleships were dispatched by the British to pursue the Russian squadron. A British admiral said he thought he only needed 4 battleships to engage the entire Russian squadron and keep the rest of his ships in reserve in order to make the Russo-British battle "fair." The only thing that prevented a war between Britain and Russia was France's intervention. France had a plan to form an anti-German block, and it didn't work out well for them when their planned allies started killing each other. Which resulted in a deal whereby Russia would surrender the officer responsible for the dogger-bank incident. Rozhestvenski took advantage of the situation to get rid of an officer he didn't like.

More blunders

After the diplomatic incident that almost triggered a war between the UK and Russia, one of the ships (the Kamchatka) had fired on a Swedish, German and French ship after mistaking those three for Japanese ships. Because of its poor gunnery, none of those ships were hit. With its anchor, one of the other ships severed the underwater telegraph cable connecting Tangier to Europe, cutting it off for five days.

The Kamchatka

In 1903, the Kamchatka was commissioned into the Russian Baltic Fleet. Her main characteristics—a sizable hold and sizable cranes—made her perfect for the job of repair ship. The ship gained notoriety for its behaviour during the Second Pacific Squadron's voyage, which helped cause the Dogger Bank incident. It was also involved in a great deal of other incidents, such as mistaking neutral ships for Japanese torpedo boats and firing at the Russian cruiser Aurora. Several ships in the fleet picked up a number of local predatory animals while docking in Madagascar, and Kamchatka was no exception. When the ship was sunk by Japanese shell fire in 1905 during the Battle of Tsushima, all of the crew members perished. The ship is also well-known in the media for its propensity to transmit the question "Do you see torpedo boats?" even when it was impossible for enemy torpedo boats to be present, causing panic on board other ships in her squadron.
